Social Engineering And The Human Factor

Context: Research paper written for CIS-199, Spring 2026. I chose this topic because I'm interested in how humans are the weakest link when it comes to security. From my research I learned how humans emotions and unpredictability makes them the most vulnerable when it comes to cyber attacks.

Social Engineering is the idea of manipulating others to give up information they shouldn’t be sharing. In context of cybersecurity this is a form of hacking but instead of hacking into security systems by brute force the hackers use various tactics to manipulate the weakest link in security systems which are human beings. There are many ways hackers will attempt to do this, and it can be done on anyone. Not only that but it’s done on a variety of scales ranging from small, targeted attacks to large company-wide attacks. The scariest part is you can have one of the most advanced and secure security systems in the world and all it would take to get through is one person knowingly or unknowingly giving the wrong person the right answers to break in.

There are a wide range of attack types and manipulation tactics that are used today. While the types are all unique all social engineering attacks include a very similar set of steps. The first step of the social engineering process is the planning phase. In this step the attacker takes the time to learn about their target. This includes looking through public records, company sites, and anything else that could be useful information to know when carrying out an attack. The next step in the process is what’s known as pretexting. Pretexting is when an attacker really starts to make their plan of attack by building a story that can help with gaining the trust of the victims. Common story devices include impersonation of trusted individuals or organizations, Pressuring victims to act before something bad happens, and taking personal information found during the planning phase and appealing to the victims in a more emotional context. Once a plan is made the next step is to act out the plan by contacting said victim or victims. This is most done through phone calls, texts, and emails but can be done in person as well. Once contact is made it is really all up to the judgement of the victim on whether the attack is successful or not. If the victim takes the bait whatever that maybe it could lead to the installation of malware, stolen information, and unauthorized access being given to the attacker. After the attacker gets what they want they can commit a number of crimes and when all is said and done, they will cover their tracks like it never happened. These steps are pretty typical amongst all social engineering attacks. There are different ways in which these steps are carried out or in other words there are different types of social engineering attacks.

Some of the most popular types of attacks include phishing, vishing, and pretexting. There are many other types, but these are just a few you’ll commonly see. Phishing is the most common because it can happen to just about anyone and it’s not a targeted attack. Phishing attacks typically happen at a wider scale and are scam messages that are sent via emails and texts. These attacks involve the attacker pretending to be a recognizable organization or even someone recognizable. The messages typically include suspicious links that when clicked may install malware or ask you to input personal information. Obviously, some of these messages are more convincing than others but it really all depends on the victims using proper judgment online. Moving on, Vishing or Voice Phishing is very similar to Phishing with the main difference being instead of messages or emails you get phone calls. These calls could be made using convincing actors or sometimes just AI voices that sound real. Vishing is among the scarier types of social engineering because if done right an attacker can really try to relate and appeal to the natural raw emotions that humans carry. For example, an attacker adding tones of urgency and fear to their voice can really catch people off guard and can be susceptible to giving up vital information. The final type I’d like to discuss is pretexting. As explained previously pretexting is a step in the social engineering process that involves making up convincing stories to tell an unsuspecting victim. While it is part of the process it is also its own type, and the entire attack revolves around the lies made up by the attacker. These attacks can happen on any platform and can even be seen in Phishing and Vishing attempts as well as real life situations. Just because most of these attacks happen behind a screen doesn’t mean they can be perpetrated in the real world. These attacks all have one thing in common and that is that they all try to appeal to kind human nature. This is what makes humans the weakest link in security.

Now what is it about us humans that make us so weak when it comes to security? The simple answer is that unlike machines we are completely unpredictable. We are all unique in our own way and come with different mindsets, emotions, and motivations. At the end of the day, we are the most dangerous weapon to ourselves. We think for ourselves and make decisions that don’t always have a positive outcome. To put it simply we make mistakes. It may be easy to say that the real target for these attacks is the older generation that aren’t as accustomed to technology as younger generations are and while old people are a huge target when it comes to social engineering that’s not to say all people under the age of 60 are immune to these scams. Social engineering attacks can be committed against anyone young and old. The best way to fight against this issue is just to be aware. We need to be able to educate ourselves on these types of scams. Being weary of strange emails, texts, and calls is also very important. And honestly just use common sense and your best judgement. Don’t click on something that seems suspicious or seems too good to be true. Don’t give out banking information to someone over the phone claiming to be your bank. It sounds a lot easier said than done. You may think that you’d never fall for stuff like that, but the truth is no one is truly safe. So the best thing you can do is always use caution. .

In conclusion, social engineering is used to manipulate people into revealing sensitive information. Typically done through messaging apps, emails, and phone calls these scammers will do just about anything to get what they want. The best way to combat our human error is just to be aware and use our best judgement. If you work for a company, make sure to follow all the security policies and just always remind yourself never to give out information that you shouldn’t. As previously said, it’s a lot easier said than done but it’s really all you can do. As humans we are the weakest link in security, and I don’t believe that is ever going to change unfortunately. And with AI on the rise these types of scams will likely get worse so we just have to be vigilant and always stay aware.
